# ACT3_BUSINESS_DESIGN.md — Run Your Own Spot (Draft v1)

> The Act 3 ambition layer: a classic management RPG where you build a Bali warung into a café. Tone: **gentle
> satire** of the Canggu scene. Locked decisions: mentor = Ibu Sari · romance = friendship for now · win =
> villa + business + bike (all three) · ~2 hrs per act.
> **Scope note:** this is the *last* big layer (after the hustle core, the 新手村 tutorial, and the social
> layer are proven). Design it now so earlier systems leave the right hooks; build it last. It can ship as a
> light "stock-and-serve" first, then deepen toward the full sim below.

## The fantasy

You stop running deliveries on someone else's app and open your own place — a tiny warung that, if you don't
go broke first, becomes the café everyone in Berawa is tagging. You're now the one dealing with suppliers who
"forgot," a barista who's on a visa run, and an influencer with 2,000 followers who'd like six free smoothie
bowls "for exposure."

## The daily management loop

**Morning:** check stock, prep the menu, set specials, schedule crew. **Service (day):** customers arrive in
waves; keep quality and speed up; handle the rush, the wifi-squatters, the one-star threat. **Evening:** count
the till, pay what's due, decide reinvest vs. save toward the villa. **Then** ride off to your other life
(hustle, crew, beach). The café keeps running in the background based on how well you set it up.

## The systems (the "all the elements" you wanted)

**1. The Spot — lease & build-out.** Sign a lease (rent + the suspiciously specific "administration" fee).
Build out the space: counter, kitchen, seating, decor, signage, an obligatory neon quote. Capacity and vibe
are stats — more seats = more covers but more rent and crew; "aesthetic" pulls the Instagram crowd.

**2. Supply & import.** Source stock two ways: the **local market** (cheap, fresh, but price swings and you
must go/send someone) vs. **imported** (the $14 wheel of "real" parmesan, the imported oat milk, the avocado
that costs more than rent) — pricey, but the bule menu demands it. Manage spoilage, bulk deals, and suppliers
who flake (the eternal "besok ya" — tomorrow). Supply quality feeds dish quality.

**3. Menu & pricing.** Choose dishes (warung staples → açaí bowls, flat whites, "is it raw vegan?"). Set
prices: the margin-vs-volume dial, plus the **two-tier tension** — local prices vs. tourist prices (a satire
beat with real reputation consequences). Specials drive traffic.

**4. Crew.** Hire — ideally **your friends from Act 2** (Kadek runs the kitchen, others on bar/service) plus
locals. Roles, wages, morale, skill/training, and scheduling (someone's always at a ceremony, a surf, or a
visa run). Happy, trained crew = faster, better service; underpaid, overworked crew = mistakes and quitting.
This is where the social layer pays into the ambition.

**5. Service & customers.** Open→serve→close, with customer archetypes that are pure Canggu: the **6-hour
laptop nomad** (one long black, owns your best table and outlet), the **influencer** (wants comps for
"exposure"), the **tourist** (orders big, tips, asks if the açaí is "ethically sourced"), the **regular**
(your bread and butter, literally), the **health-conscious crowd** (vegan/GF/"no seed oils"). Demand swings by
time, weather, and nearby events. Satisfaction → reviews.

**6. Finances & overhead.** A real P&L: **rent, wages, utilities, supplies, "administration," taxes**, vs.
revenue. Cash flow matters — a bad week + payroll can sink you. Each evening: reinvest (upgrades, menu, crew)
vs. bank it toward the villa. Going under = back to the hustle to recover (no hard game-over; you can rebuild).

**7. Reputation & reviews.** Ties straight into the existing venue rating system: a **Google-style star
rating** that gates footfall, **influencer visits** that can spike or tank you, and word-of-mouth in your
social graph. One viral TikTok = a flood you may not be staffed for; one bad health inspection = a crisis.

**8. Growth & events.** Once stable: host nights (open-mic, run-club breakfasts, a supper club), collabs with
other venues, a second location — the early metaverse/commerce seed (your events later promote into the shared
world). Growth is the bridge from "surviving" to "a name."

**9. Risk & random events** (keeps it lively, very Bali): health inspection, **wet season** killing footfall,
a **viral spike** you must scramble to staff, a **competitor** opening next door with a nicer sign, equipment
breaking mid-rush, a crew member on a sudden visa run, a power cut, a "manifestation circle" booking the whole
patio. Each is a small decision with money/reputation stakes.

## How it ties to the arc

- **Hustle (Acts 0–1)** earns the **capital** to open — the grind finally has a destination.
- **Crew (Act 2)** become your **staff** — friendships convert into a working team.
- **Reputation** reuses the existing rating system — your driver rating's cousin.
- **The villa fund** runs in parallel (bank profit toward it); the **bike** is the third leg. **All three** =
  the Act 4 win + multiplayer unlock.
- **~2-hour pacing:** Act 3 is open → stabilize → grow to "known," roughly two hours of management play, tuned
  by the money economy set in earlier acts.

## Build scoping (honest)

- **v1 (light):** lease + build-out + a small menu + local supply + 2–3 crew + the service loop + basic P&L
  (rent/wages/supplies) + the star rating. Proves the loop is fun.
- **v2 (deepen):** import supply, pricing tiers, morale/training/scheduling, influencers, growth/events, the
  full random-event deck.
- **Done-for-now** = you can open a spot, run it at a profit, grow its rating, and bank toward the villa, with
  enough texture that the Canggu satire lands.

Build it **after** the core loop, tutorial, and social are proven — but leave hooks now: the crew system should
reuse Act 2 relationships, and the café should be a venue that reads the existing rating/reputation data.

## Satire, kept affectionate

The joke is *with* the scene, not at anyone — the $9 smoothie bowl, the exposure economy, the imported-avocado
crisis, the besok-ya supplier, the new café every week, the wifi-squatter who's "basically a regular." Warm,
recognizable, a little exasperated. Never mean.
